## Overview

This session bridged philosophy, neuroscience, and technical AI to ask a single question:

> *Can a machine dream?*

Rather than treating AI as purely technical infrastructure, the workshop examined generative AI as a mirror for understanding human creativity itself.

## Structure

**1. Conceptual Foundation**
Framed human creativity through Aristotle's tripartite soul model and four pillars â€” consciousness, emotion, subjectivity, and intentionality â€” as a benchmark for evaluating AI systems.

**2. How AI Creates**
Technical breakdown of modern generative systems: LLMs (token prediction), diffusion models (iterative denoising), GANs (adversarial training), and music generation models. Core observation: AI generates via statistical pattern synthesis, not subjective experience.

**3. Theory of Mind & The Dream Question**
Drew on Premack & Woodruff (1978) to examine whether current models possess self-awareness, autobiographical identity, or belief. Conclusion: AI recombines learned structures â€” it does not dream in any biological or phenomenological sense.

**4. Interactive Exercise**
Participants compared a human-written and AI-generated poem, often unable to distinguish them. This prompted discussion of meaning vs mechanism, experience vs simulation, and whether authorship matters if emotional response is identical.

**5. Co-Creation & Ethics**
Reframed AI as amplifier and creative assistant rather than autonomous collaborator. Covered authorship, deepfakes, bias, environmental cost, and AI governance (EU AI Act).
